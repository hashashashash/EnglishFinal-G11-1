Guernica (1937) - Pablo Picasso

Ursula K. Le Guin's "The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas" and Pablo Picasso's "Guernica" are bitter commentaries on the hidden violence of “order” as both artworks starkly illustrate how societies regularly condemn the vulnerable for the greater good of their own social order—Omelas's utopian happiness is entirely maintained by the unseen torture of a suffering child locked deep in the cellar, while Guernica's tumultuous black-and-white composition commemorates the slaughtered innocents of a Basque town who died to pave the way for fascist regimes—but both artworks present a scathing indictment of humanity for ignoring such suffering as necessary by using the few characters in Le Guin's work that reject Omelas's social contract by walking away from it through Picasso's artwork as a manifestation of collective grief for suffering suppressed by society as seen in Guernica's intertextual representation of 'stifled pain' or grief.

Visual Elements

  • Monochrome Palette: Black, white, and gray evoke the starkness of war, grief, and moral decay.
  • Chaos & Suffering: Distorted bodies, screaming faces, a wounded horse, and a mother clutching her dead child fill the canvas—each figure representing the innocent victims of violence.
  • Symbolism: The broken sword (defeat of humanity), the lightbulb (a harsh spotlight on injustice), and the open-eyed bull (brutality and indifference) reinforce the painting’s critique of cruelty.

Invisible Victims, Visible Comfort

Omelas:

"The child is put in a cellar beneath the city and never seen again."

Omelas’ inhabitants live their utopian life while forgetting about and turning a deaf ear to this suffering child. According to Le Guin, "All the prosperity and beauty and delight of Omelas would wither and be destroyed" were it not for this "necessary" suffering of this child.

Guernica:

The bombed civilians included women, children, and the elderly; they offered their lives for political statements. It is very strange that Picasso painted these faces because they cannot be ignored. The cries and agonies fill up the painting.

Complicity vs. Resistance

Omelas:

Its people know about the suffering of the child. But they accept it. They cry, they are angry—but then they obey as if there is no other choice. Some people “walk away,” leaving their home.

Guernica:

In Picasso’s artwork, there is a defiance against what is happening. Through his artwork, he shows people a picture of the aftermath of the bombings and refuses to allow the world to turn a blind eye to such acts. His artwork is a symbol of anti-war messages.

The Illusion of “Necessity”

Omelas:

The happiness of the city is based on a lie. The child has to suffer; his misery is "necessary." But Le Guin shows that the lie is that the choice lies between utopia and chaos, not justice and cruelty.

Guernica:

The bombing of Guernica was framed as a “military necessity” by the Nazis and their allies. Picasso’s work dismantles this excuse, showing that violence against civilians is never “necessary”—it is a choice.
